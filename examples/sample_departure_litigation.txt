The American Lawyer - December 2025

LATHAM LITIGATION PARTNER DEPARTURE SPARKS $8M LAWSUIT

A high-stakes litigation has erupted after Thomas Reynolds, a senior litigation partner at Latham & Watkins for 23 years, departed to join Paul Weiss in September. The case highlights emerging concerns about data security during partner transitions at elite law firms.

According to the complaint filed in U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York (Latham & Watkins LLP v. Reynolds, Case No. 1:25-cv-8847), Reynolds allegedly engaged in "systematic and premeditated theft of client relationships and confidential firm data" over a six-month period preceding his departure.

Court documents reveal a detailed forensic timeline:

DIGITAL FORENSICS FINDINGS:

Six months before departure (March 2025):
- Reynolds began forwarding emails from 47 key client contacts to personal ProtonMail account
- Created personal Dropbox account and began syncing litigation strategy documents

Four months before departure (May 2025):
- Downloaded complete client contact database (2,847 entries) to personal laptop
- Accessed firm's billing system after hours, exported rate cards and fee arrangements
- Searched internal database for "client satisfaction scores" and "relationship mapping"

Two months before departure (July 2025):
- Increased client entertainment spending by 340% (later characterized as "pre-departure cultivation")
- Began meeting clients outside normal business contexts
- Printed 3,200+ pages including case strategies, expert witness lists, and settlement authorities

Final month (August 2025):
- Copied entire litigation playbook folder (78GB) to external drive
- Forwarded 156 confidential case memos to personal email
- Deleted browser history and cleared email sent folder

"The scale of preparation was unprecedented," said Latham's managing partner in a statement. "This wasn't opportunistic - it was a calculated six-month operation."

FINANCIAL IMPACT:

Court filings detail the alleged damages:
- 23 clients followed Reynolds to Paul Weiss (representing $9.2M annual billings)
- Confidential rate information allegedly used to undercut Latham's pricing
- Two pending matters worth estimated $15M in fees moved mid-stream
- Firm estimates $2.1M in forensic investigation and remediation costs

SECURITY GAPS EXPOSED:

Industry experts note the case reveals common blind spots:

1. EMAIL MONITORING: Latham's security systems flagged external forwards but auto-approved forwards to "known" personal accounts registered to partners

2. CLOUD STORAGE: No restrictions on partner-level cloud sync; personal Dropbox installed on firm equipment without alerts

3. PRINTING PATTERNS: No anomaly detection for printing volume; 3,200 pages went unnoticed

4. CLIENT CONTACT ACCESS: Partners had unrestricted export capabilities for client databases

5. AFTER-HOURS ACCESS: No behavioral triggers for unusual access patterns despite 340% increase in weekend logins

6. DEVICE MANAGEMENT: Personal laptops used for firm work without MDM controls

DEFENSE RESPONSE:

Reynolds, through counsel, denies wrongdoing: "Mr. Reynolds maintained client relationships through decades of personal service. Clients chose to follow him - as is their right and his. The so-called 'exfiltration' is simply normal partner work product development."

His attorneys argue the information was not trade secret material and that non-solicitation provisions in his partnership agreement are unenforceable under New York law.

SETTLEMENT TALKS:

Sources indicate preliminary settlement discussions valuing the dispute at $8-12M, though significant gaps remain between the parties.

INDUSTRY IMPLICATIONS:

Legal technology consultants say the case will drive changes:

"Every AmLaw 100 firm should be asking: do we monitor email forwarding rules for partners? Do we track cloud storage sync? Do we have printing anomaly detection? Most don't," said Jennifer Walsh, a legal security consultant.

Latham has since implemented:
- Real-time monitoring of email forwarding rules at all levels
- Cloud storage restrictions for equity partners
- Behavioral analytics on document access patterns
- Mandatory exit forensics for all departing partners
- "Garden leave" policy with 90-day paid transition

The case is scheduled for a preliminary injunction hearing January 15, 2026.
